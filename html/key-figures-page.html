Marie
Taglioni
Information sourced from Britannica
Born: April 23, 1804 in Stockholm, Sweden
Died: April 24, 1884 in Marseille, France
Marie Taglioni was an Italian ballerina trained by her father, Filippo Taglioni, and was the most distinguished ballerina of her time. Her elegant dancing which was described to extend from the stage to the audience’s soul characterized the Romantic style in the early 19th century. When she performed in her father’s ballet La Sylphide, in March 1832, she became the first ballerina to perform a full routine en pointe. Her dancing en pointe developed a new style within ballet dance including jumps and sustained balances en pointe, such as an arabesque. Not only did she make key innovations in the emergence of pointe, but the costume, tight bodice and skirt, she wore in this ballet pushed toward the iconic tutu we associate with ballet today. Marie Taglioni made great innovations in ballet and pointe, being classified as one of the greatest classical ballerinas.

Fanny
Elssler
Information sourced from Britannica
Born: June 23, 1810 in Vienna, Austria
Died: November 27, 1884 in Vienna
Fanny Elssler was an impressive ballet dancer, from her performance to technique, who is credited with the introduction of character dance in ballet. She trained in ballet from her childhood and began performing at the Paris Opéra in 1834. She became famous from her Polish Folk dance in La Gypsy, 1839, as well as her introduction of Spanish cachucha in Le Diable Boiteux, 1836. Her rival was Marie Taglioni, each having individual performance qualities. Marie was known for her elegance while Fanny was known for her enthusiasm. Due to this, when she attempted to dethrone Marie as the greatest classical ballerina, she performed in La Sylphide, but failed due to her opposing dance quality. In 1840-1842 she toured the United States and broke her contract with the Paris Opéra to continue touring around the world and eventually retired in 1851.

Carlotta
Grisi
Information sourced from Britannica
Born: June 28, 1819 in Visinada, Istria, Austrian Empire
Died: May 29, 1899 in Saint-Jean, near Geneva, Switzerland
Carlotta Grisi was an Italian ballerina during the Romantic era known for creating the title role in Giselle as well as merging the opposing dance qualities of Marie Taglioni and Fanny Elssler. From a young age Carlotta trained in ballet, but since her family was poor she was taken out of school and at 10 years old joined a touring opera company. In 1834 she joined the ballet company of the San Carlo Naples where Jules Perrot shaped her into the successful ballerina she became. At the Paris Opéra her first creation was the title role for Giselle in 1841 and was the principal ballerina there until 1849. She also performed in many ballets in London during this time. The last leg of her career was in St Petersburg between 1850-1853 while Jules Perrot was ballet master. Carlotta Grisi’s last performance was in Warsaw in 1853.

Marius
Petipa
Information sourced from Britannica
Born: March 11, 1818 in Marseille, France
Died: July 14, 1910 in Gurzuf, Ukraine, Russian Empire
Marius Petipa was a successful dancer and choreographer at the Mariinsky Theatre in St. Petersburg that influenced modern ballet, especially in Russia from 1870-1903. Marius’ ballet training traces back to his father Jean Petipa, who was a ballet master. His first performance at the Mariinsky Theatre was in the 1847 performance of Paquita. Nearly ten years later, in 1858, he presented his first original ballet Un Mariage sous la Régence (“A Regency Marriage”). He became choreographer in 1862 then promoted to chief choreographer in 1869. Many of the ballets that he created are still performed today! He collaborated with Tchaikovsky on popular ballets such as, The Nutcracker, The Sleeping Beauty, and versions of Swan Lake and Giselle.

Serge
Diaghilev
Information sourced from Britannica and Victoria and Albert Museum
Born: March 31, 1872 in Novgorod province, Russia
Died: August 19, 1929 in Venice, Italy
Serge Diaghilev was an arts advocate who created a lasting impact in ballet who revitalized the love for ballet, modernized it and organized the Ballet Russes in 1909. From a young age he loved the arts, graduating with a law degree, yet wanting to pursue music. Due to his personal art influences he opened the Ballet Russes in 1909. The choreography in the company was unique and innovative, creating a new quality of movement in ballet. He wanted to create new ballets, hiring many different choreographers, composers, and dancers to accomplish this dream while still having total control over his productions. His company toured in areas around the world, including America and Europe, rekindling the interest in ballet in these countries. Serge Diaghilev is credited with making lasting impacts in ballet following his death, creating a modern classical ballet unlike anything before.

Anna
Pavlova
Information sourced from Britannica
Born: January 31, 1881 in St. Petersburg, Russia
Died: January 23, 1931 The Hague, Netherlands
Russian ballerina Anna Pavlova was the most famous ballerina of her time. Her most renowned solo she performed was The Dying Swan in 1905 created for her by Michel Fokine. Her movement quality was very moving leaving audiences with lasting impacts from her performances. She began her ballet training at the Imperial School of Ballet in 1891, joining the Imperial Ballet in 1899, and was promoted to prima ballerina in 1906. In 1909, she joined the Ballet Russes tour and went on to dance in New York City and London in 1910. Starting in 1914, Anna Pavlova independently toured, travelling around the world. She was very interested in studying dance techniques from all around the world.

Karen
Kain
Information sourced from Britannica and The National Ballet of Canada
Born: March 28, 1951 in Hamilton, Ontario, Canada
Karen Kain is a prominent Canadian ballerina at the National Ballet of Canada in addition to the longest-serving Artistic Director at the National Ballet, 2005-2021, since the founder. Karen Kain trained in ballet at Canada’s National Ballet School, joining the Corps de Ballet of the National Ballet of Canada in 1969. A few years later, in 1971, she became principal dancer after her performance as Odette/Odeile in Swan Lake. In 1973 her and Frank Augustyn competed at the International Ballet Competition in Moscow where the duo was awarded with Best Pas De Deux and Karen won the silver medal in the Women’s Category. Karen Kain performed in various ballets and companies and retired from dancing in 1997. In 2005 she became Artistic Director of the National Ballet and from 2004-2008 she was Chair of the Canada Council for the Arts. In 2007 a school was named after her; the Karen Kain School for Arts. One of her most distinguished awards was the Queen Elizabeth II Coronation Award in 2019, being the first Canadian to receive this honor.

Misty
Copeland
Information sourced from Britannica and the Misty Copeland Website
Born: September 10, 1982 in Kansas City, Missouri, U.S
Misty Copeland is an American ballerina who became the first African American female principal dancer at the American Ballet Theatre. Misty’s ballet training began at age 13, which is quite late for a ballerina of such impressive skill as Misty. She trained at San Francisco Ballet School and American Ballet Theatre's Summer Intensive. She joined the Studio Company of the American Ballet theatre in 2000 and became a member of the Corps de Ballet in 2001. In 2007 she became the second African American Soloist in the company’s history. Continuing to make history, in 2015 she became the first African American female principal dancer at the American Ballet Theatre and in 2014 she was the first black female to perform as Odette/Odile in American Ballet Theatre’s Swan Lake. She stands for making ballet more diverse, continuing to be a great example for young ballerinas, even becoming a best-selling author.
